## The Challenge

A defense software organization was operating under traditional waterfall development processes. Planning happened in large batches, feedback cycles were long, and teams had limited visibility into work in progress. As the organization recognized the need to modernize its development practices, individual teams needed to prove that agile methodologies could work within the constraints of a defense environment, where process rigor and predictability are non-negotiable.

The challenge was not simply adopting agile. It was finding the right methodology for each team's actual workflow, rather than forcing every team into the same framework regardless of how their work was structured.

## The Approach

The transformation unfolded across three phases, each addressing a different process bottleneck. Planning efforts, process documentation, and decision rationale were captured in Confluence throughout, providing a living record that other teams could reference.

### Phase 1: Waterfall to Agile

The team pioneered the transition from waterfall to agile within their group, establishing sprint ceremonies, backlog management, and iterative delivery practices. This was not a top-down mandate; the team led the change from within and demonstrated its effectiveness. The transition was successful enough that it became the reference implementation for other teams across the organization looking to adopt agile practices.

### Phase 2: Scrum Optimization

With agile established, the team turned its attention to making Scrum work more effectively. Ceremonies were refined, and the team led daily standups, backlog refinements, retrospectives, and planned demonstrations. Collaboration with Scrum Masters and Product Owners helped identify dependencies and align on future priorities. This phase was about getting the most out of the Scrum framework before deciding whether it was the right long-term fit.

### Phase 3: Targeted Kanban Adoption

Through the optimization work, the team identified that Scrum's fixed sprint structure was not the best fit for every team. Some teams were shoehorning planning activities and sprint ceremonies into workflows that did not naturally align with fixed-length iterations, wasting time on process overhead rather than producing output. For teams where this pattern was identified, the team championed a shift to Kanban methodology, focusing on continuous flow, work-in-progress limits, and pull-based prioritization. To support the new workflow, Jira issue collectors and Jira Service Desk were configured to allow the broader organization to submit requests and bugs that were automatically converted into Jira issues and placed directly into the Kanban board. This eliminated manual intake processes and ensured that incoming work entered the workflow immediately.

## Results

| Metric                         | Before                          | After                                                |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Development methodology        | Waterfall                       | Agile (Scrum or Kanban, fit to team)                 |
| Issue open time (Kanban teams) | Baseline (Scrum)                | 25% reduction                                        |
| Work intake                    | Manual                          | Automated via Jira issue collectors and Service Desk |
| Team output                    | Constrained by process overhead | Increased through right-fit methodology              |
| Organizational adoption        | One team                        | Transition used as reference across the organization |

The three-phase transformation demonstrated that agile adoption is not a one-size-fits-all event. The initial waterfall-to-agile shift established modern development practices and became the model for the broader organization. Optimizing Scrum ceremonies ensured the team extracted full value from the framework before moving beyond it. And the targeted shift to Kanban for teams whose workflows did not fit fixed sprints showed that the right methodology depends on the nature of the work. Teams that moved to Kanban increased their overall output and reduced issue open time by 25%, validating that the goal was never to adopt a specific framework but to find the process that best served each team's delivery.
