## What separates legitimate from sketchy

Vague, interchangeable messaging like "We help businesses grow through innovative solutions" is the hallmark of a site with nothing real behind it. ## The real name, real photo, and "we" vs "I" question

**Use your real name and a professional headshot — this is non-negotiable for a solo consultant.** Real photos outperform stock images by **35% in conversion rates** according to MarketingExperiments research. More importantly, the absence of any identifiable person is the single biggest red flag that signals a shell company. Your photo proves someone real exists behind the LLC.

The "we" versus "I" debate generates strong opinions in consulting communities. The practical answer for a solo DevOps LLC is the **hybrid approach**: use your company brand name on the homepage and services pages ("CloudForge Solutions helps scaling startups..."), then write the About page in first person ("I'm [Name], the founder of CloudForge Solutions..."). This gives you the credibility of a company while maintaining the authenticity of a personal brand. Whatever you choose, be ready with a confident answer when a prospect asks if you're solo — they often do, and hesitation creates distrust.

For privacy-conscious consultants, the headshot does permanently associate your face with the business. But the trade-off is clear: no photo means dramatically less trust from potential clients. If you're doing legitimate consulting, this is a necessary step.

## Handling NDA-bound work and anonymous case studies

This is the most common challenge for DevOps consultants, and the good news is that **anonymized case studies can actually be more compelling than named ones**. Without a recognizable logo doing the credibility work for you, you're forced to be specific and detailed about the actual problem, approach, and results. You can also be more candid about how bad things were before you arrived, since no one is being embarrassed.

Five proven strategies for presenting confidential work:

- **Use descriptive identifiers**: "A Series B SaaS startup with 50 engineers" or "A Fortune 500 healthcare organization" instead of client names. Include industry, company size, revenue range, or team size — these are rarely NDA-restricted.
- **Lead with specific metrics**: "Reduced deployment time from 2 hours to 8 minutes," "Cut monthly AWS spend by 42% ($18K/month savings)," "Migrated 47 microservices to Kubernetes in 3 months." Numbers are your strongest weapon when you can't use logos.
- **Focus on technical detail**: Describe the architecture, tools, challenges, and approach in depth. Technical readers care more about your methodology than the client name. This is a natural advantage for DevOps consultants.
- **State the anonymization explicitly**: A line like "This case study has been anonymized at the client's request" reassures both current and prospective clients that you take confidentiality seriously — which actually builds trust.
- **Aggregate results across projects**: "Across 12 cloud migration projects, I averaged 35% cost reduction and 99.95% uptime" tells a powerful story without referencing any single client.

Check your NDA terms carefully — most allow discussing the existence of a project, the technologies used, your role, and your general results. Many have sunset clauses expiring 6 months to 2 years after project completion.

## Domain names, email, and digital infrastructure

**Get a .com domain** — this remains the most trusted extension for business credibility with enterprise clients. The **.io** extension is also widely accepted in the tech/DevOps space. Avoid .org [NamePros](https://www.namepros.com/threads/on-choosing-a-domain-name-for-a-consulting-business-website.1287229/) (people assume nonprofit), avoid hyphens (confusing verbally), and don't include "LLC" in your domain name — it's a legal designator, not a brand element.

You have two strong options: **yourname.com** works best if you're building a personal brand [Consulting Success®](https://www.consultingsuccess.com/consulting-domain-name) and getting referrals by name. **brandname.com** works best if you might eventually grow, [Consulting Success®](https://www.consultingsuccess.com/consulting-domain-name) bring on subcontractors, or sell the business. For a DevOps consultant, including a technical indicator in your brand name (something with "cloud," "infra," or "ops") helps signal your niche immediately. Secure the domain before finalizing your LLC name — many consultants pick an LLC name only to find the .com is taken.

**Domain-matched email is essential.** Using john@yourcompany.com instead of a personal Gmail account is one of the simplest and most impactful credibility signals. One consultant coach put it bluntly: "Using @gmail.com makes you look like an amateur, even if you're the best consultant on the planet."

## Whether a blog helps and when it hurts

A technical blog is one of the most powerful credibility builders for a DevOps consultant, but **a blog with one stale post from six months ago actively hurts your credibility more than having no blog at all**. Only start a blog if you can commit to publishing at least monthly.

The alternative for launch: create **3-5 strong evergreen articles** and hide the publication dates. [Brilliantauthor](https://brilliantauthor.com/articles/consultant-website-must-haves) Topics that work well for DevOps consultants include architecture decision guides ("When to use ECS vs. EKS"), cost optimization strategies ("How to reduce your AWS bill by 30%"), lessons-learned narratives from real projects, and tool comparisons. Each piece should feel like a valuable mini-consulting session — demonstrate your thinking and judgment without giving away step-by-step implementation details.

One consultant reported that organic search traffic from blog content drives **at least half of their annual consulting revenue**. [Tsavo Neal](https://tsavoneal.com/best-consultant-websites/) For DevOps specifically, technical content ranks well because engineers actively search for solutions to infrastructure problems. Writing about Terraform best practices, Kubernetes architecture decisions, or CI/CD pipeline design positions you as exactly the expert they need when they realize the problem is bigger than a blog post can solve.

If you do blog, consider also linking your **GitHub profile** from your site. For DevOps consultants, an active GitHub with infrastructure-as-code examples, open-source contributions, or sample configurations serves as a technical portfolio that complements your case studies.

## Putting it all together

The consulting website that wins isn't elaborate — it's specific, authentic, and professionally executed. Build the five core pages with real specifics about your work, use your actual face and name, match your email to your domain, display your certifications prominently, and write 2-3 anonymized case studies packed with metrics. Cross-link everything with your LinkedIn profile (keep messaging consistent between both) and consider a simple LinkedIn Company Page as an additional static touchpoint.

For platform choice, **Squarespace** offers the fastest path to a polished site for non-designers. **WordPress with a clean theme** (Astra or GeneratePress) gives better SEO and flexibility for long-term growth. Some DevOps consultants build static sites deployed on AWS or Netlify as a subtle demonstration of technical capability — a nice touch if you enjoy it, but not necessary.

The most important insight from practitioners is this: **launch simple and iterate**. A clean four-page site with strong messaging goes live this weekend. Case studies, blog posts, and testimonials accumulate over time. The website that exists and looks professional beats the perfect website that's still in planning six months from now.
